# AI Tells: Structure

Use this module when the words are individually acceptable but the prose still feels generated.

## Meta-Argument Scaffold

The prose narrates stance, contrast, or importance before giving the claim.

Tells:

- "This is not X, but Y"
- "The real frontier is..."
- "The second concern is..."
- "The argument now turns..."
- "At its core..."

Check: if a sentence says "not X," point to where X was claimed. If you cannot, cut the contrast.

Before:

> This is not a story about algorithms replacing economists, but about the changing location of bottlenecks.

After:

> Automation reduces the time spent cleaning survey responses. Variable definitions and sample restrictions still determine the estimate.

## False Concession

AI uses "while" and "although" to sound balanced without weighing anything.

Before:

> While automated grading is impressive, real-world classroom deployment remains a challenge.

After:

> Automated grading can score multiple-choice exams at scale, but it still misses partial-credit reasoning in open-response math work.

## Self-Labeled Significance

The prose tells readers how to react.

Tells:

- "the interesting part"
- "the key insight"
- "the quiet scandal"
- "the contrarian move"
- "what matters most"
- "read that again"

Before:

> The key insight is that the compliance burden moves through the system.

After:

> Small employers report wages, payroll vendors encode the rule, and the revenue agency still bears enforcement risk.

## Rhetorical Question Openers

Questions often stall before the point.

Before:

> So what does this mean for urban housing policy?

After:

> The binding constraint shifts from zoning approval to financing the required sewer and transit capacity.

Keep a rhetorical question only when the setup genuinely earns it and the answer is not obvious.

## Parenthetical Hedging

Asides can fake nuance without committing.

Tells:

- "(and, increasingly, X)"
- "(or, more precisely, Y)"
- "(and perhaps more importantly, Z)"

Fix: if the aside matters, give it a sentence. If it does not, cut it.

## Treadmill Effect

The paragraph restates the premise in fresh words instead of advancing it.

Test: ask "what is new here?" for every paragraph. If the answer is only "same thesis, different wording," cut or rebuild.

Before:

> The public role remains important. Public actors continue to matter because they provide important public resources that support the broader innovation system.

After:

> Public microdata give researchers a shared baseline, but privacy rules still determine which household-level variables can be linked.

## Paragraph-Reshuffle Test

Could two body paragraphs swap places without breaking the logic? If yes, the piece may be a list of points dressed as an argument.

Fix at the structural level:

1. Name the controlling claim.
2. Give each paragraph one new move.
3. Make each move depend on the previous one.

## Rhythm Uniformity

AI prose often bends different ideas into the same grammar.

Tells:

- three or more sentences with the same length
- repeated "X does A, while Y does B, so Z does C"
- repeated three-item groupings
- repeated em-dash asides
- repeated aphoristic closers

Before:

> Transmission studies identify bottlenecks, while auctions set prices, so regulators shape the middle layer. Developers build projects on this substrate, while utilities retain the operational ends, so durable advantage remains concentrated.

After:

> Transmission studies identify where the grid is congested. Auctions set the price of new capacity. Regulators matter for a different reason: they decide which upgrades enter the rate base. Developers can build projects around that process, but they still do not control utility interconnection queues or dispatch rules.

## Synonym Cycling

Human writers repeat the clearest word. AI often rotates synonyms to avoid repetition.

Before:

> Developers, practitioners, builders, and engineers all need better tools.

After:

> Researchers need better tools.

Repeat the right noun when precision matters.
