# Figures and Captions

Use this module for captions, diagrams, charts, maps, tables, schematics, figure callouts, and prose that keeps describing a visual instead of returning to the subject.

## Caption Job

A strong caption is not a mini-essay. It should do four things:

1. Name what is mapped or measured.
2. Define marks, colors, axes, rings, categories, or other encodings.
3. State the visible pattern in concrete terms.
4. Give one evidence rule or scope limit if needed.

Then stop.

## Avoid Figure Self-Commentary

Do not say what the figure "argues," "states," "reveals," "claims," or "proves." Say what it maps and what pattern is visible.

Before:

> The geometry states the section's claim visually; its single defensible point is structural, and by omission it marks the two ends as absent.

After:

> Representative organizations mapped by public evidence of activity across seven capability layers. Filled marks indicate strong evidence; outlines indicate partial or emerging activity. Coverage is broad in model and compute layers and narrower where proprietary data or closed-loop validation is required.

## Remove Process Leakage

Finished prose should not refer to prior layouts, removed titles, drafts, comments, prompts, or revision history.

Flag:

- "redrawn from the earlier grid"
- "with its title removed"
- "title-stripped"
- "this revised version"
- "as requested above"
- "the figure now"
- "the omitted actors"

Before:

> With its title removed, the figure reads as a map of origins, not a ranking.

After:

> The figure groups organizations by origin and capability.

Before:

> Redrawn from a row-and-column matrix, the same actors now form concentric rings.

After:

> The actors are arranged as concentric rings, one per capability layer.

If the reader would ask "compared to what?" and the answer is "a draft you never saw," rewrite.

## Do Not Repeat The Caption In Body Prose

The caption defines the visual encoding and names the visible pattern. Body prose should return to domain nouns: companies, datasets, instruments, policies, experiments, bottlenecks, consequences.

Before:

> On the wheel, these firms trace the same spoke shape, thin through the middle rings and thick again at the two ends.

After:

> These firms reach the full system from the opposite direction: they already own the data and validation loop, then buy or build the middle tools around them.

One body callout is usually enough:

> Figure 2 gathers the suppliers just named.

Then return to the argument.

## Avoid Negative-Space Claims When Direct Claims Work

"By omission, the figure marks X as absent" is usually weaker than "none of the organizations shown owns X."

Before:

> By omission, the map marks the two end layers as unoccupied.

After:

> None of the organizations shown owns both the proprietary experimental record and the production validation loop.

## Scope Notes: Positive, Not Defensive

One scope note may be necessary. Several in a row sound defensive.

Before:

> The set is illustrative, not exhaustive; vendor claims are attributed, not endorsed; Company X is one node, not the center.

After:

> Placement reflects public products, papers, and documented deployments.

Before:

> This is not a ranking and should not be read as an endorsement.

After:

> Organizations are grouped by capability, not ordered by performance.

Use "not" when it names a real boundary. Avoid it when it rebuts a suspicion the prose created.

## Figure Recursion Test

After editing a caption and the following paragraph, check:

- Does the caption define the visual once?
- Does the body prose avoid repeating the visual pattern?
- Are visual nouns replaced by domain nouns after the first callout?
- Is the evidence rule stated once, not defended repeatedly?
- Does the figure reduce explanatory burden?

If the prose keeps explaining the figure's mechanics, either the figure is unclear or the prose does not trust it.
